# Resolving Conflict
_If you plan to best the Deep Woods, you'll need more than ready fists..._

## You Fight With Your Soul
Every player starts the game with `100d6`, and that's the highest point they'll ever achieve in this regard. From the minute play begins, players can only ever lose dice. Sure, there are ways to recycle some of the spent dice back into your possession, but there's an entropy to the system. **Slowly, but eventually, all the dice will slip into the void**. One hundred dice sounds like a lot, until suddenly you're nearly out.
<br><br>
Every time you make oppose your surroundings in DD1D, you pay with a little bit of yourself. Sometimes you change what you are - you give up a limb, or an emotion, or a bit of your future - but other times you give up your *you-ness*, how much of you you're still in control of. The former currency is found exclusively on your Character Sheet (you can check it out on the next tab), but the latter is represented by your **dice pool**. Every time you attempt to overcome great odds, you pull from your dice pool and become a little less *you*, so make sure you're fighting for things that really matter! Players may choose how many dice they'll commit to any given action, but once they've settled on a number those dice *must be used*. If you are playing IRL, I recommend having players keep all their dice in a bag, or a box, or a bucket - something that can be closed. When a player takes dice out of their container, those are the dice they're using, so they must be mindful not to be flippant with their idle hands.   

### Measuring Success
When the outcome of an action is uncertain, players will roll some dice. Dice have three possible outcomes:
<br><br>
* **GOOD**: The result is a 4, 5, or 6. Good results are tallied and if there are enough to meet the threshold set by the GM then the result of the player's attempt ultimately achieves what they're after.
* **BAD**: The result is a 1 or a 2. Bad results do not stop players from achieving their goals, but they change an unmitigated success to a "yes, but..." when rolled, and if a player rolls more bad results than good results then they suffer dearly from their attempt. In the latter case, the GM will [BIND] a new detail to the player's character sheet to represent their folly. More about that in the **"Character Sheet Instruction"** tab.
* **BECOMING**: The result is a 3. If any number of 3s are rolled, the player must fill in one of the **Transformative Moments** boxes below the dice results. This occurs before the result of the player's attempt is announced. What this means, and how to proceed, are both described in greater detail in the Character Sheet instructions.

Players may choose to roll any number of dice when attempting to overcome a challenge, no matter how big or small. When rolling multiple dice, players roll all dice at the same time and add up all their Good / Bad / Becoming results. Players increase the number of Good results tallied by +1 if they rolled more than one 4. They increase it by another +1 if they rolled more than one 5, and another +1 if they rolled more than one 6. For instance, rolling `4, 4, 5, 5, 5, 6` would give a player 8 good results: six from the values rolled, then +1 for rolling multiple 4s and another +1 for rolling multiple 5s. It doesn't matter how many of each number you roll, the bonus will only ever be +1 per number.

The same modifier is true for Bad results. If a player rolls multiple 1s they increase their Bad results by +1, and if they roll multiple 2s they do the same. Rolling multiple 3s has no effect.

The number of Good results a player needs to achieve their goal is determined by the kind and difficulty of the conflict at hand... 

### Unopposed Conflict
Most of the time, characters will be struggling to overcome their surroundings or their own mental / physical state. Though these kinds of challenges may be *against* something, they're usually not against someone else with a dice pool, so they're unopposed conflicts.
<br><br>
The vast majority of the time a character tries to do something they're well equipped for, or naturally good at, they'll only need a single Good result. If a character is attempting something they *could* do, by having helpful experience because of [WHAT YOU WERE] or if [WHAT YOU HAVE] could be used in a round-about way to help, then the GM will set a threshold number that the player will try to match. Requiring three Good values would represent a moderate challenge, while five or more would be a strenuous challenge. 
<br><br>
If a character has no relevant details on their Character Sheet for the challenge at hand, or if they did not ante for a Scene (more on this in the **"Map Generator Instructions"** tab), then they'll need to try with *Wild Abandon*.  

### Wild Abandon
If a character wants to try something wholly outside of their personal experience, or something that no mortal being would be suited to do, they'll need to give a dice to the GM for every dice they want to cast. For instance, if a player wants to roll 5 dice with Wild Abandon, they'll need to give 5 dice straight from their dice pool to the GM. The donated dice are not rolled - effectively, players need to spend twice as many dice as normal if they have no details related to the challenge they're facing. 

### Opposed Conflict
In some circumstances, the characters will face *challengers* - NPCs controlled by the GM or sometimes other characters. In these circumstances, the threshold for success is more fluid and is wrapped up in a tug-of-war between the two parties. This happens most often during Combat, but not always. The aggressor starts by rolling any number of dice, and then tallies their Good and Bad results as normal, but this time they subtract the Bad from the Good. If they have any Good results remaining, they successfully challenge the other party - whether by striking them, or imposing their will on them, etc. - otherwise they fail right from the start and suffer dearly for their attempt. 
<br><br>
This is only the beginning, however, as the opposing party now gets a chance to respond. They must now roll as many Good results as the aggressor in order to stay in the fight. If they do, then they successfully avoid the aggressor's hit and the fight continues with the aggressor now trying to roll as many Good results as the defender has just rolled. If the defender fails, then they've lost the fight and suffer dearly for it (often times, and NPC will die from losing a fight, but a PC will just become injured).
<br><br>
Fighting in this way may go on as long as the parties continue to oppose each-other, but any party may stop rolling at any time to forfeit the conflict. Remember, you have limited dice, so sometimes it's just not worth it to fight back, especially if the opposing party is not a genuine threat. Also remember that you may roll as many dice as you wish for any conflict, so if you want the fight to be over quickly, consider escalating your force at a pivotal moment.

### We Give And Take Our Dice
In DD1D, you can only overcome challenges, or oppose others, if you have dice to cast - this includes the GM, who starts the game with `0 dice`. Instead of starting with a pool, the GM eats (gains) the dice that players cast and ante. Every time a player joins a Scene, or attempts to overcome a challenge, they are feeding the GM resources to oppose them in the future. When the GM uses dice, however, they are sent to either the **Cache Pool**, a reserve that players can pull from when they bargain for more dice or encounter a Cache Location, or they are sent to the **Void Pool** - essentially, they are removed from the game entirely.

When a GM has no dice in their pool, they cannot set a threshold of difficulty when a player attempts to resolve a conflict (the default threshold is 1 Good result). When a *player* has no dice in their pool, their character can no longer maintain their sense of self. They become subsumed by the Deep Woods, they `lose autonomy and become an antagonist alongside the GM`. The player will assist the GM with storytelling and dice rolling until the game is over or they're revived via deus ex machina.

### Changing the Pool Without Casting
From time to time, players will need to remove or add dice to their pool without casting them. To do this in the digital companion, use the "Update pool" button to add dice back into the pool (used when recovering dice from a Cache Location), or just use the "Ante Dice" button to remove dice without rolling them (used when casting with Wild Abandon).
